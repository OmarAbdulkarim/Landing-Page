AI in construction — practitioner's notes

The new tool
in the toolbox.

This is not a vendor pitch. I'm a project controls analyst who learned AI, put it to work on live projects, and trains my team to use it well. What follows is what actually earns its keep on a construction program — and, just as important, what doesn't.

Where it earns its keep

Input → Output → The catch

/ 01

Claims & correspondence drafting

IN   delay events · window analysis results · clause references
OUT  structured first-draft EOT narrative & contractual letters

Drafting that took days now takes hours. The analysis stays mine — the delay is proven in the program, not by the model. Every fact in the draft gets verified against the schedule before it goes anywhere.

/ 02

Code on demand

IN   a plain-English spec of a controls task
OUT  working VBA · Power Query · dashboard logic

This is how tools on this site got built at speed — describe the check, the transformation, the output, and iterate on working code. A planner who can spec a problem clearly can now build the tool that solves it.

/ 03

Program narratives

IN   variance tables · KPI exports · schedule health results
OUT  management-ready progress commentary

The weekly report's first draft writes itself from the numbers. The analyst's job moves from typing to editing — checking emphasis, adding context the data can't see, owning the conclusion.

/ 04

Contract & spec navigation

IN   a clause or spec section · a plain question
OUT  a plain-language reading, with references to verify

A navigation aid for FIDIC and project specs — faster than paging through PDFs. Never a substitute for reading the clause itself, and never legal advice. AI points; the engineer reads.

/ 05

Team enablement

IN   a planning team · their real weekly workflows
OUT  planners who use AI safely and check its work

I train my team on live work, not theory — the workflows above, the ground rules below. The goal isn't AI enthusiasts; it's analysts whose output got faster without getting sloppier.

Where it doesn't

Knowing the limits is the skill

✕

CPM math and float calculations

Scheduling is deterministic. P6 computes the critical path; a language model guesses at it. Never ask AI to calculate what a scheduling engine calculates — ask it to explain, summarize, or draft around results you already trust.

✕

Anything you can't verify

Models fabricate clause numbers, standards references, and plausible-sounding figures. If you can't check the output against a source you control, the output is a liability, not a deliverable.

✕

Confidential project data in public tools

Programs, rates, and claim strategies are commercially sensitive. Nothing confidential goes into a tool without an appropriate data agreement — anonymize, aggregate, or keep it local.

✕

Replacing judgment

AI drafts the letter; it doesn't decide whether to send it. Which delay to claim, how to sequence a recovery, what to concede in negotiation — that's the analyst's job, and signing your name to unreviewed AI output is how credibility dies.

Prompt patterns for planners

A prompt is a spec — write it like one

PATTERN 01 — Delay narrative from analysis results
You are drafting a section of an EOT claim. Using only the facts below, write a chronological delay narrative for [event]. Do not invent dates, durations, or causes. Where a fact is missing, insert [VERIFY]. Facts: - Baseline finish of activity A1040: [date] - Instruction [ref] received: [date] - Time-impact analysis result: [X] days on the critical path - Affected activities: [list]

Why it works: the model gets facts, not freedom. The [VERIFY] instruction turns hallucination risk into a visible checklist.

PATTERN 02 — Tool spec to working code
Write an Excel VBA macro. Input: a sheet named "XER_TASK" with columns [list columns]. Output: a new sheet listing every activity with total float above 44 days, sorted descending. Rules: skip completed activities; convert float from hours to days using column [x]; format the output as a table. Ask me questions before writing if anything is ambiguous.

Why it works: inputs, outputs, rules, edge cases — the same way you'd brief a junior. "Ask before writing" catches bad assumptions early.

PATTERN 03 — Update summary for management
Summarize this progress data for a monthly report to senior management. Audience: non-planners. Length: 150 words. Lead with overall status, then the two most significant variances, then the key risk for next period. Neutral tone — no adjectives the data doesn't support. [paste variance table]

Why it works: audience, length, structure, and tone are pinned down. The model formats; the data speaks.

Ground rules I train on

Faster without sloppier

Verify against the source, every time

AI output is a draft, not a deliverable. Every date against the program, every clause against the contract, every figure against the export. If you didn't check it, you didn't do it.

Privacy before productivity

Decide what's shareable before opening the tool, not after. Program data, rates, and claim positions stay out of public models. When in doubt, anonymize or leave it out.

Prompts are specs

Vague in, vague out. Inputs, outputs, constraints, format — brief the model the way you'd brief a junior planner, and demand questions back when the brief is unclear.

Start with the boring wins

Formatting, first drafts, code snippets, summaries. Build the verification habit on low-stakes work before pointing AI at a claim. Trust is earned by the workflow, not by the tool.

Own the output

Whatever leaves your desk carries your name, not the model's. AI is leverage on judgment — never a substitute for it.
